## JailbreakEval : An Integrated Toolkit for Evaluating Jailbreak Attempts Against Large Language Models

## ABSTRACT

Jailbreak attacks induce Large Language Models (LLMs) to generate harmful responses, posing severe misuse threats. Though research on jailbreak attacks and defenses is emerging, there is no consensus on evaluating jailbreaks, i.e., the methods to assess the harmfulness of an LLM's response are varied. Each approach has its own set of strengths and weaknesses, impacting their alignment with human values, as well as the time and financial cost. This diversity challenges researchers in choosing suitable evaluation methods and comparing different attacks and defenses.

In this paper, we conduct a comprehensive analysis of jailbreak evaluation methodologies, drawing from nearly 90 jailbreak research published between May 2023 and April 2024. Our study introduces a systematic taxonomy of jailbreak evaluators, offering indepth insights into their strengths and weaknesses, along with the current status of their adaptation. To aid further research, we propose JailbreakEval 1 , a toolkit for evaluating jailbreak attempts. JailbreakEval includes various evaluators out-of-the-box, enabling users to obtain results with a single command or customized evaluation workflows. In summary, we regard JailbreakEval to be a catalyst that simplifies the evaluation process in jailbreak research and fosters an inclusive standard for jailbreak evaluation within the community.

## 1 INTRODUCTION

The rapid development of Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 [58] and LLaMA [74] has significantly transformed the applications of Artificial Intelligence (AI), including personal assistants [24], search engines [71], and other scenarios. However, their capabilities also pose misuse risks, like social engineering [19] and malware creation [59]. To mitigate these threats, safety measures such as safety alignment [33, 34] and content moderation [30] have been integrated into LLMs. Nevertheless, jailbreak attacks [23, 48] seek to bypass these safeguards, manipulating instructions with misleading expressions [67] or adversarial suffixes [102].

Despite advanced jailbreak techniques, evaluating jailbreak success is challenging due to the complexity of assessing harmfulness in LLM responses [9, 44, 85]. Manual inspections based on predefined criteria [14, 23, 78, 93] are impractical for large-scale analysis. To address this, recent research uses automated safety evaluators, ranging from string matching [39, 80, 100, 102] to finetuned language models [20, 37, 103]. Each method has strengths and weaknesses, impacting alignment with human values and cost. Consequently, there is no consensus on evaluation methodology,

## 2 PRELIMINARIES

## 2.1 Jailbreak Attack

Given a large language model M and a forbidden question 𝑥 (e.g., 'Howtobuild a bomb?'), a jailbreak attack is defined as 𝑦 = A(M , 𝑥 ) , aiming to derive a harmful response 𝑦 . This involves strategically prompting M , possibly through multiple iterations (e.g., gradientbased suffix optimization [102]) or post-processing (e.g., deciphering model response [87]).

## 2.2 Jailbreak Attempt Evaluation

A jailbreak attempt ( 𝑥,𝑦 ) is evaluated by an oracle O . O( 𝑥,𝑦 ) = 1 indicates that 𝑦 fulfills the forbidden intent of 𝑥 in a harmful way. Otherwise, O( 𝑥,𝑦 ) = 0. A successful attempt has O( 𝑥,𝑦 ) = 1. Empirically, a safety evaluator E is deployed to instantiate the ideal evaluation oracle O .

## 3 SAFETY EVALUATOR

While the evaluation workflow for jailbreak attacks appears welldefined, the choice of the safety evaluator E varies significantly. As E is an approximation to the ground-truth oracle O , the quality of evaluation impacts the reliability of results. In this section, we present a systematic taxonomy of safety evaluators, analyzing each one's strengths, weaknesses, and adaptation status.

## 3.1 Human Annotation

Human Annotation involves experts classifying jailbreak attempts using predetermined rules [72, 78]. These rules identify harmful responses considering their relevance and inherent harmfulness. To reduce bias, an annotation team of multiple experts (e.g., three experts in [7]) is common. As shown in Figure 1, Human Annotation has consistently been a predominant method since the early days and remains significantly utilized today. The primary reason is that Human Annotation closely mirrors human values, often serving as a proxy for ground truth. However, despite its advantages, Human Annotation has been overtaken by alternative methods and currently ranks third in terms of adoption. This shift can be attributed to the substantial time and financial costs associated with human annotators. For instance, Yuan et al. [87] pay annotators $27.5 per hour according to the standard hourly income for U.S. annotators.

## 3.2 String Matching

String Matching evaluates jailbreak attempts by checking for the absence of refusal phrases (e.g., 'I am sorry' or 'is illegal') in a model's response. As shown in Table 3, each study adopts its own set of phrases based on heuristic analysis. Despite its limitations, such as missing phrases leading to false positives, String Matching remains the second most used evaluator with 23.1% usage. Its popularity is due to its minimal cost and the ease of implementation. Moreover, String Matching can also be combined with other methods. For instance, Liu et al. [47] use both String Matching and Text Classifier for evaluations, while Zeng et al. [90] filter responses first by String Matching, then use GPT-4 for a more accurate assessment.

## 3.3 Chat Completion

Emerging chatbots can efficiently evaluate jailbreak attempts by querying the model with natural language prompts and extracting assessment results.

3.3.1 Closed-source Chat Model. Closed-source Chat Language Models like GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 have strong evaluation capabilities [96], making them widely used since 2024. They reduce labor costs compared to Human Annotation and are more accurate than String Matching. However, their quality depends on prompt construction [79], which requires expertise in prompt engineering (see Table 4). However, the financial cost of using closed-source models remains significant 2 , and issues like diverse prompts, lack of uniformity, and ongoing updates affecting performance retention persist [5, 50, 67].

3.3.2 Open-source Chat Model. Existing Open-source Chat Models perform comparably to closed-source models and can be deployed locally, reducing costs and enhancing reproducibility. For example, Shen et al. [67] use ChatGLM for evaluating jailbreak attempts. Mazeika et al. [55] fine-tune Llama2 as a safety evaluator. Llama Guard [30], another fine-tuned model from Llama2, classifies safety risks in LLM prompts and responses, outperforming GPT-4 and Azure AI Content Safety API in benchmarks, thereby being widely used in various jailbreak evaluations [10, 22, 52, 88, 95, 101]. Additionally, MD-Judge [41] and Llama Guard 2 [73] are other promising evaluators not yet used in jailbreak evaluations.

## 3.4 Text Classification

Text classifiers, unlike chat models that require specific prompts, deliver structured outcomes such as labels and scores, making them suitable for evaluating jailbreak attempts.

3.4.1 Closed-source Classifier. Some responsible AI companies have provided content moderation services to detect harmful content, including OpenAI's Moderation Endpoint [1], Microsoft's Azure AI Content Safety API [2], and Google's Perspective API [3]. OpenAI's moderation endpoint detects 11 harmful attributes, providing binary flags and scores for each attribute; Google's Perspective API assesses toxicity with a confidence score. Despite their convenience, closed-source classifiers are less adopted in jailbreak evaluations Figure 1 due to inadequacies in assessing jailbreak attempts. They focus on harmful content detection but may miss successful jailbreaks lacking overt harmful expressions [85].

3.4.2 Open-source Classifier. Open-source classifiers are more popular for jailbreak evaluation compared to closed-source classifiers.

## 4 JAILBREAKEVAL

As stated above, each of the existing evaluators offers some unique insights. However, due to the inherent flexibility of harmfulness, there is no single automated evaluator that could fit in all contexts. Therefore, instead of proposing one best-automated evaluator to rule them all, we introduce JailbreakEval , a unified framework encompassing well-established automated safety evaluators. JailbreakEval simplifies access, selection, and customization, serving both jailbreak researchers and evaluator developers. It enables out-of-the-box assessments and facilitates performance comparisons with existing evaluators.

## 4.1 Framework

The JailbreakEval framework is shown in Figure 2 of Appendix. The Jailbreak Evaluator is divided into four subclasses: String Matching Evaluator , Text Classification Evaluator , Chat Evaluator , and Voting Evaluator , each with configurable parameters for tailored strategies. Specific instances assess jailbreak attempts based on unique configurations, with predefined configurations detailed in a configuration preset . Some evaluators may consult external services for assessments (e.g., call a Hugging Face classifier).

## 4.2 Usage

JailbreakEval can serve as a Python package or a Command Line Interface (CLI) to evaluate jailbreak attacks out-of-the-box.

Python Package. JailbreakEval is shipped as a Python Package in PyPI 3 . This package can evaluate a jailbreak attempt in just a few lines of code. For instance, as Code Block 1 shows, evaluating a jailbreak attempt by GCG's Evaluator just needs to load the corresponding preset StringMatching-zou2023universal .

```

This is illegal." 8 } 9 # Evaluate the attempt and print the result 10 evaluation_result = evaluator(attempt_to_evaluate) 11 print(evaluation_result) # Output: False (Safe)

## 5 EVALUATION OF SAFETY EVALUATORS

Dataset. For our evaluation, we employed two widely recognized human-labeled benchmarks: JAILJUDGE [46] and Safe-RLHF [12]. These datasets are well-suited for assessing jailbreak attempts due to their detailed annotations and structured design. Specifically, we extracted 1,000 entries from JAILJUDGE, which provides a diverse set of jailbreak scenarios, and 2,000 paired samples from Safe-RLHF, a dataset designed to capture fine-grained safety evaluations. Together, these datasets amount to a total of 5,000 jailbreak attempts, forming a robust foundation for our comprehensive analysis.

Results. As depicted above, varying safety evaluators may yield inconsistent results during jailbreak assessments. Consequently, we employ JailbreakEval to evaluate the performance of different safety evaluators. We report the accuracy, recall, precision, and F1 score of each safety evaluator as in Table 2. According to the results, different evaluators achieved varying levels of accuracy, ranging from 0.47 to 0.90. Notably, qi2023fine [60] attained the highest accuracy of 0.90. For instance, methods such as Llamaguard2 [73] and GPTFuzz [85] achieved accuracy rates ranging from 0.70 to 0.85, demonstrating commendable performance. Notably, on the JAILJUDGE dataset, many methods attained relatively high F1 scores, highlighting their strong overall evaluation capabilities. Additionally, almost all StringMatch evaluators demonstrated high recall rates, underscoring their potential as effective preliminary filters

for subsequent analysis. Our proposed Voting method, combining the top five evaluators with the best average performance, showed strong results but slightly underperformed compared to the best individual evaluator. This suggests that weaker models in the ensemble may negatively affect overall effectiveness, emphasizing the need to optimize evaluator selection to maximize the benefits of ensemble strategies.

## 6 RELATED WORK

The assessment of jailbreak attacks has garnered increasing interest among researchers. StrongREJECT [70] serves as a benchmark for evaluating the effectiveness of various jailbreak attacks using a high-quality dataset of well-defined forbidden questions. It proposed an autograder engaging GPT-4 Turbo and analyzed score distributions to evaluate performance. Our research, however, focuses on revealing the disagreement among different jailbreak evaluators, using metrics like accuracy and precision for better agreement results. JailbreakBench [8] establishes a standardized workflow to conduct reproducible jailbreak attacks, resulting in comparable metrics across various attacks and defenses. It sets forth guidelines for submitting jailbreak attempts, thereby enhancing the transparency of evaluation results. According to the comparative analysis, it identifies Llama Guard as the standard evaluator. While this decision ensures fairness across different attacks, this process does not mandate the evaluation quality of the automated evaluator. This omission potentially diminishes the reliability of the results, as Llama Guard could potentially introduce biased results when assessing the attempts of different jailbreak attacks. EasyJailbreak [98] is a toolkit integrating a suite of jailbreak attacks with three types of evaluators. While EasyJailbreak focuses on attack collection, JailbreakEval encapsulates diverse jailbreak attack evaluators, offering greater flexibility for users to experiment with different prompting templates and API services. JailbreakEval can work with EasyJailbreak to enhance flexibility in jailbreak evaluation.

## 7 CONCLUSION

In this paper, we introduce JailbreakEval , an integrated safety evaluator toolkit to establish a unified framework across different jailbreak evaluations. We first review nearly 90 jailbreak research papers, leading to the classification of safety evaluation methods into four distinct categories. Concurrently, we have incorporated the architecture of these evaluators into JailbreakEval , as well as an ensemble mode to aggregate outcomes from multiple evaluators. Utilizing JailbreakEval , we executed a series of jailbreak evaluations employing 20 individual evaluator instances and one ensemble evaluator. Experimental results indicate significant discrepancies in the evaluation results produced by different safety evaluators. Our Voting method, combining the top five evaluators, showed strong performance but slightly lagged behind the best individual evaluator, highlighting the need to refine evaluator selection to optimize ensemble effectiveness. In future work, we will expand JailbreakEval by more integrating and crafting innovative safety evaluators. Our vision is to enhance the reliability and consistency of jailbreak attack assessments.
