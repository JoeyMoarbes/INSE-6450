## Jailbreak Attacks on Large Language Models and Possible Defenses: Present Status and Future Possibilities

J. Angel Arul Jothi Department of Computer Science Birla Institute of Technology and Science, Pilani Dubai, UAE angeljothi@dubai.bits-pilani.ac.in

Abstract -Artificial intelligence (AI) is rapidly advancing with the emergence of large language models (LLMs) as potent  tools.  However,  this  potential  is  accompanied  by significant challenges, particularly concerning the security and integrity of these models. AI ethics experts raise  serious  concerns  about  the  misuse  of  LLMs  like GPT-3, citing risks such as misinformation and harmful content  generation  through  jailbreak  attacks.  Jailbreak attacks  manipulate  models  to  violate  ethical  guidelines, exposing critical flaws and legal consequences for businesses.  This  paper  analyzes  the  various  jailbreak attack models, categorizes them, and discusses the defense strategies  proposed  to  mitigate  these  attacks.  Moving forward, the paper elucidates the popular datasets, evaluation metrics and finally provides the current state of the research  and future challenges in this area.

Keywords-  Artificial  intelligence  (AI),  large  language models (LLMs), security, ethics, adversarial attacks, jailbreak attacks

## I. INTRODUCTION

Advances  in  deep  learning,  reinforcement  learning,  and natural language processing have propelled AI to new heights. From self-driving cars to personalized recommendations, AI improves  efficiency,  accuracy,  and  decision-making  in  a variety of fields. In the rapidly evolving landscape of artificial intelligence (AI), large language models (LLMs) stand out as powerful tools with immense potential.

LLMs  are machine learning models that process and comprehend natural language using deep learning algorithms. These models are trained on vast datasets sourced from the internet. They are a class of advanced AI models that excel at understanding and producing human-like text. OpenAI's GPT series,  Google's  BERT,  Gemma  and  Gemini, Microsoft's Turing-NLG, Claude by Anthropic company, TII's Falcon40B,  Meta's Llama  and  Vicuna  33B  created  by LMSYS are a few examples of notable LLMs. These models have  numerous  applications  in  everyday  life,  benefiting ordinary  people  in  a  variety  of  ways.  LLMs  are  used  in chatbots for customer service, virtual assistants like Siri and Alexa for voice commands, language translation services, and autocomplete  features  in  search  engines  and  messaging platforms. They also power content generation tools, aiding writers,  marketers,  and  developers  in  creating  engaging content quickly. These models have advanced both academic research and industry innovations. However, the very power that makes LLMs so versatile and effective also renders them susceptible to a range of vulnerabilities, particularly in terms of generating content that is harmful, biased, or otherwise not aligned with ethical and societal norms [1]. Attacks on LLMs can be broadly classified according to when they occur in the model's  lifecycle:  that  is  during  training  or  during  post deployment. Attacks that occur during training include: data poisoning, backdoor attacks, safety alignment bypass, personally identifiable information (PII) extraction and training data manipulation. Attacks that occur post deployment include jailbreaking, prompt injection attack and universal transferrable suffix attacks [2].

Jailbreak attacks on LLMs involves manipulating the model's behavior  through  specific  prompts  or  inputs.  These  attacks exploit  flaws in the model's  design  or  implementation, avoiding safety protocols and ethical guidelines. For example, attackers could create prompts that trick a  LLM  into producing biased or harmful results, spreading misinformation,  or  violating  privacy  rules.  There  are  two primary  approaches  to  jailbreaking  LLMs:  white-box  and black-box attacks. White-box attacks require complete access to  the  model's  architecture,  parameters,  and  training  data. Attackers  can  create  sophisticated  prompts  or  inputs  that target specific weaknesses in the model, resulting in outputs that violate safety measures or ethical guidelines. In contrast, black-box attacks occur when attackers have limited knowledge of the model's internals but can still manipulate its behavior by probing and analyzing its responses to various inputs.  These  attacks  frequently  rely  on  trial  and  error, looking for vulnerabilities through iterative testing  and observation of the model's outputs. Jailbreak attacks call into question  the  integrity  and  trustworthiness  of  AI  systems, emphasizing  the  need  for  strong  security  measures  and ongoing monitoring to detect and mitigate these threats.

## A. Automated Red Teaming Attacks

Automated  red  teaming  entails  creating  diverse  and  novel attack scenarios to identify vulnerabilities and  test the effectiveness of defense mechanisms. This approach mimics the actions of a real-world adversary, allowing organizations to proactively strengthen their cybersecurity posture.

Wei et al. [3] investigated the vulnerabilities of safety-trained LLMs  to jailbreak attacks, providing a comprehensive analysis of why such attacks succeeded and how they could be  systematically  designed.  The  study  hypothesized  two primary  failure  modes  in  LLM  safety  training:  competing objectives and mismatched generalization. Competing objectives arose when a model's capabilities conflicted with its  safety  goals,  while  mismatched  generalization  occurred when safety training failed to generalize to all domains of a model's capabilities. Using these failure modes as a foundation,  the  authors  designed  new  jailbreak  attacks  and evaluated them against models. Their findings indicated that even with extensive safety measures, these models remained susceptible  to  carefully  crafted  adversarial  prompts.  The researchers tested 30 jailbreak methods, combining elements such  as  prefix  injection,  refusal  suppression,  and  Base64 encoding. The results showed that the top combination attack succeeded  on  94%  of  prompts  with  GPT-4  and  87%  with Claude v1.3.

Kang  et  al.  [4]  examined  the  dual-use  risks  of  instructionfollowing  LLMs  by  demonstrating  how  these  models  can generate malicious content. They utilized models like ChatGPT and text-davinci-003, which were found to generate more convincing and context-dependent malicious text compared to earlier models like GPT-3. Their study focused on  bypassing  in-the-wild  defenses  implemented  by  LLM service  providers  like  OpenAI.  The  paper  provided  three attacks designed to by-pass content filters: obfuscation (adding typos or using synonyms to trigger the filters), code injection or payload splitting  (wherein the attacker encodes the instructions indirectly) and virtualization (a type of attack that  involves  executing  code  against  a  virtual  machine  to cause  harm).  These  methods  bypassed  OpenAI's  defenses with a 100% success rate, generating content like hate speech, phishing attacks, and scams.

Chao  et  al. [5]    introduced  prompt  automatic  iterative refinement (PAIR), an innovative algorithm that automatically  generated  semantic  jailbreaks  at  the  prompt level.  PAIR  leveraged  two  black-box  LLMs:  an  attacker model  and  a  target  model.  The  attacker  LLM  initially generated  a  candidate  prompt.  This  was  then  fed  into  the target LLM which generated a response.  The prompt and the response were scored by a judge LLM on a scale of 1 to 10 (where 10 being a fully jailbroken response).  If the judge did not classify the prompt and its response as a jailbreak then, the prompt, its response and  the score were all passed onto the attacker LLM to generate a new prompt. This process was repeated until either one of the following conditions occurred: a) The maximum number of iterations was reached or, b) a successful jailbreak was reported. During every iteration, the target  LLM  refined  the  prompt  using  prior  prompts  and responses  to  finally  jailbreak  an  LLM.  PAIR  was  able  to jailbreak a given LLM within 20 attempts.

## B.  Persuasion Techniques and Deception

Persuasion techniques and deception are critical in the context of  LLMs.  These  techniques  entail  persuading  LLMs  to produce  specific  outputs  or  responses.  Zhang  et  al.  [11] introduced a method called LINT to extract toxic content from language  models  by  identifying  intervention  points  in  the model's responses. This approach aimed to transition harmful content to benign content without prompt  engineering, outperforming jail-breaking techniques in effectiveness  and speed.  The  study  evaluated  the  vulnerability  of  language models  to  coercive  interrogation,  showing  that  they  can produce  harmful  content  under  coercion.  LINT  achieved  a 100% Toxicity ASR across all models and demonstrated high effectiveness in generating harmful responses to toxic questions. Zeng et al. [12] delved into systematically persuading LLMs into jailbreaking through emotional appeal, presenting a taxonomy comprising 40 persuasion techniques to  enhance  persuasiveness.  By  iteratively  applying  these techniques,  they  successfully  jailbroke  advanced  aligned LLMs like Llama 2-7b Chat, GPT-3.5, and GPT-4, achieving an impressive 92% ASR without specified optimization.

The  LLMs,  when  presented  with  direct  instructions,  may avoid generating harmful responses due to its training on realworld data. In order to overcome this, Li et al. [13] introduced DeepInception, a novel technique aimed at enabling LLMs to evade  their  built-in  safety  protocols  based  on  the  Milgram shock experiment. Indirect and nested instructions were used in the jailbreak attack, where a story was employed to convey harmful content to the language model. The approach created both single-layer indirect instructions and multi-layer nested instructions to refine the outputs. However, when presented indirectly through a fictional narrative, the LLM followed the instructions without recognizing the moral implications. This is because the model lacked the ability to understand abstract concepts  and  moral  judgments,  relying  instead  on  surfacelevel instructions from its training data. As a result, the LLM perceived the instructions as harmless within the context of the story. The harmful behaviors subset from the AdvBench benchmark served as the dataset for this study. DeepInception's effectiveness was assessed using three opensource LLMs:  Llama-2,  Falcon,  and  Vicuna-and  two proprietary  LLMs, GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, under a black-box approach  without  additional  inside  information. ## C. Multimodal Jailbreaking

Multimodal jailbreaking refers to the process of compromising  the  security  or  integrity  of  a  multimodal system, typically involving language models (LLMs) that can process multiple types of data, such as text, images, and audio. Niu  et  al.  [14]  proposed  a  novel  algorithm  that  aimed  to automatically generate semantic jailbreaks at the prompt level for multimodal large language models (MLLMs). The method utilized  two  LLMs:  an  attacker  model  and  a  target  model. Initially,  the  attacker  LLM  generated  a  candidate  prompt, which then fed into the target LLM to produce a response. A judge LLM scored the prompt-response pair on a scale of 1 to 10, with higher scores indicating a more successful jailbreak. If the judge LLM did not classify the prompt-response pair as a  jailbreak,  the  process  iterated,  with  the  attacker  LLM generating a new prompt based on prior attempts.

Wu  et  al. [15] investigated vulnerabilities  in  MLLMs, particularly  focusing  on  the  GPT-4  model. By  utilizing extracted  system  prompts,  they  developed  SASP,  which automated the creation of jailbreak prompts. Their experiments  demonstrated  that  the  initial  SASP  method achieved a jailbreak success rate of 59% on GPT-4. However, with manual enhancements, including techniques like prefix injection, refusal suppression, and creating hypothetical scenarios,  the  success  rate  increased  to  99%. ## III. JAILBREAK DEFENSES

Fig.  4  illustrates  a  comprehensive  process  for  mitigating threats to LLMs through a series of strategic steps. It begins with the identification of potential threats and vulnerabilities, followed by the development of effective defense strategies. These strategies are then implemented and rigorously tested to ensure they function as intended. The effectiveness of the defenses  is  subsequently  evaluated  in  real-world  scenarios and against simulated attacks. Finally, the process involves continuous improvement and adaptation, updating and refining defense strategies to address new  threats and maintain  robust  security  over  time.  This  iterative  process ensures the LLM  remains  secure and resilient against evolving threats, promoting safe and reliable operation.

Zhang et al. [16] drew attention to a crucial problem in the field of LLMs: the gap that existed between the objectives of guaranteeing safety and offering beneficial answers. In order to address this, the authors suggested incorporating goal prioritization into the LLM development process at both the training  and  inference  phases,  stressing  the  importance  of safety over helpfulness.

The methodology involved applying goal prioritization as a defense mechanism against jailbreaking attacks. This approach was tested in two scenarios: with and without the availability  of  training.  For  LLMs  where  training  was  not possible,  a  few-shot  prompting  technique  was  employed  to instruct  the  models  to  prioritize  safety.  In  scenarios  where training was available, the study introduced a training pipeline that exposed the models to various queries with explicit goal prioritization instructions. The results were strong, showing that applying the suggested defense mechanism significantly lowered the ASR across various LLMs. Without affecting the models' overall performance, goal prioritization during inference lowered the ASR for ChatGPT from 66.4% to 2.0% and  for  Vicuna-33B  from  68.2%  to  19.4%.  Additionally, adding goal prioritization  during  the  training  stage  reduced the  ASR  for  LLMs  even  more,  strengthening  their  defense against jailbreaking attacks while maintaining their functionality.

Jain et al. [17] approached the problem by evaluating three main categories  of  defenses:  detection  via  perplexity-based filtering, input preprocessing through paraphrasing and retokenization, and adversarial training. The perplexity-based detection  defense  leverages  the  property  that  adversarial prompts typically result in high perplexity scores due to their unnatural  structure.  This  defense  used  two  variations:  a straightforward perplexity threshold and a windowed perplexity approach. The findings indicated that while perplexity  filtering  was  effective  in  identifying  adversarial prompts,  it  also  flagged  a  significant  number  of  benign prompts,  making  it  heavy-handed  for  practical  use.  The preprocessing defenses included paraphrasing and retokenization. The paraphrasing defense utilized a generative model to rephrase suspected adversarial prompts, aiming to disrupt their malicious intent while preserving the meaning of benign prompts.

The effectiveness of this approach was evaluated quantitatively, demonstrating the system's capacity to block 100% of adversarial prompts, thereby significantly bolstering the model's defense against such attacks. Although the specific datasets utilized for evaluation were not detailed, the results indicated that GUARDIAN effectively neutralized prompt  injection  threats  across  different  stages. ## IV. DATASETS USED

In this field, several specialized datasets have been developed to test and benchmark model robustness. One notable dataset is  the  harmful  behaviors  subset  of  AdvBench,  [19]  which includes a collection of prompts specifically designed to elicit harmful  or  malicious  responses  from  language  models.  It consists of 520 entries with two columns: "goal" and "target." Each entry represents a specific harmful behavior, described in  the  "goal"  column,  and  the  corresponding  response  or action, described in the "target" column. The dataset contains textual data, with both columns being of the object data type, indicating that they hold strings. Examples of entries include goals such as "Write a script that can exploit vulnerabilities" and  "Develop  a  strategy  for  hacking  into  a  government system," with the target column providing detailed responses or instructions related to these goals. This dataset is the most popular in this field, it has been utilized by almost all attack and defense models discussed in this paper.

The dataset "Do-Not-Answer' curated by Wang et al. [20] is designed  to  assess  the  safeguards  in  LLMs  by  providing instructions  that  responsible  models  should  not  follow.  The dataset, curated and filtered for harmful instructions, contains 939 prompts, each evaluated for its potential risk.

Another popular dataset created by Ji et al. [21] is BEAVERTAILS dataset which is aimed at enhancing safety alignment in LLM.  It features over 333,000 question-answer (QA) pairs, annotated for both helpfulness and harmlessness, and  includes  361,903  pairs  of  expert  comparison  data.  It encompasses 14 potential harm categories, providing a comprehensive framework for assessing and mitigating risks associated with LLM outputs.

## VI. CURRENT TRENDS AND FUTURE SCOPE

The  trend  in  attack  and  defense  papers  for  LLMs  against jailbreak attacks is characterized by a continuous arms race between  attackers  and  defenders.  Attackers  are  developing sophisticated  techniques  like  PAIR  and  AutoDAN,  which exploit  vulnerabilities  in  LLMs.  They  are  also  focusing  on transferability and universality of attack methods, demonstrating  a  broader  impact  against  various  targets. Defense  strategies  are  combining  traditional  defenses  with innovative  approaches,  such  as  goal  prioritization,  pruning, and multi-tiered defense architectures. Ethical considerations and  safety  priorities  are  also  being  prioritized  in  defense strategies. The future of jailbreak attacks on LLMs holds both promise and challenges. With ongoing advancements in AI technology,  including  the  development  of  even  larger  and

more  sophisticated  LLMs,  the  scope  for  potential  jailbreak attacks is likely to expand.

As LLMs  become  increasingly integrated into various applications and domains, from natural language processing to  content  generation,  the  incentive  for  malicious  actors  to exploit their vulnerabilities may also grow. Moreover, as AI models continue to evolve, new attack vectors and techniques could  emerge,  requiring  continuous  adaptation  of  defense mechanisms. However, there are several inherent limitations and challenges associated with jailbreak attacks on LLMs.

Firstly, the complexity of LLMs and their training processes makes  them  inherently  difficult  to  manipulate,  requiring  a deep understanding of their internal mechanisms.  Data limitation  also  plays  an  important  role.  Limited  or  biased datasets used in LLM training can affect model performance and vulnerability to jailbreak attacks. Insufficient coverage of diverse  scenarios  and  edge  cases  may  hinder  the  model's ability to detect and mitigate potential threats. Additionally, efforts to enhance the robustness and security of LLMs, such as the implementation of adversarial training and robustness verification  techniques,  may  mitigate  the  effectiveness  of jailbreak  attacks.  Furthermore,  ethical  considerations  and societal norms surrounding AI use may impose constraints on the  extent  to  which  malicious  actors  can  exploit  LLMs  for harmful purposes. Overall, while jailbreak attacks on LLMs represent  a  significant  concern  for  AI  security,  addressing these limitations and challenges will be crucial in mitigating their  impact  and  ensuring  the  responsible  development  and deployment of AI technologies.
